Rotational Motion: Movement in Circle

Written on 20th of November, 2024

Many terms in Physics sound the similar but mean different things due to the subtleties. After the study of linear motion and the familiarization to Newton's various laws of inertia, I want to take the time to decorticate rotational motion, clarifying the nuances in the jargon and establishing a solid foundation that will equip us for more advanced speculations.

What is an Angle?

Before we tackle rotation, we first need to understand its elementary foundation which is an angle. An angle is a portion of a circle. “Measuring an angle” is assigning a value to that circular portion. The thing is, an angle does not appear magically though, it starts somewhere-just like anything in life has a beginning and also an end.

Let’s say you decide to take a walk on a circle. You will first stand at a particular spot, then start walking. Let’s call that starting point A. As you start walking, you realize that you need a break and stop for a second-this is position B—that’s our position B.

We then define angle \(\theta\), the portion of the circle you walked from A to B.

Degrees vs Radians

Angles can be expressed in either degrees or radians. Which one is better is context-depend. Usually we use degrees to have an intuitive feel for the angle we’re describing in contrast to radians which is convenient for formula derivation and to avoid bulky numbers through the use of \(\pi\).
